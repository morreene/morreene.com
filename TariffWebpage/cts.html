TARIFFS

WTO IDB and CTS Databases

The WTO has two main tariff databases - the Integrated Database (IDB) and the Consolidated Tariff Schedules (CTS).

  • Integrated Database (IDB) contains time series of official applied tariffs and matching imports of WTO members and acceding economies at the most detailed product level (usually referred to as the eight or more-digit product or tariff line code).
  • Consolidated Tariff Schedules (CTS) contains the commitments or the agreed maximum level of duty that can be imposed on imported products from other WTO Members.

CTS - History 

The CTS project, approved by the WTO Committee on Market Access (CMA) on 27 November 1998, was aimed at improving the situation regarding the recording of members' concessions on goods. It involved the formatting, consolidation and standardization of members' tariff commitments (bound tariffs) and specific commitments in agriculture (domestic support, export subsidies and tariff quota information).

Developed members prepared their own CTS files while the Secretariat prepared the files for all developing members. The files were then reviewed by the members concerned and, where relevant, comments and clarifications introduced, after which the files were deemed approved. Members have the right to review and amend their CTS files at any time.

Rectifications and modifications to schedules are continuously introduced in the CTS. A major update was the ITA and ITA-Expansion commitments for the participating members. The results of the Nairobi package to eliminate export subsidies have also been reflected in the CTS files.Â  CTS files for newly acceded members have been created. Corrections and revisions identified by members and the Secretariat are also being incorporated in the CTS files. Revised files are made available to download from TAO as they are processed, and the delegations concerned informed accordingly.

The original version of the CTS was recorded in the HS1996 nomenclature but for most members their commitments have now been successively transposed to HS2002, HS2007 and HS2012 nomenclatures. For each transposition exercise the Secretariat prepared notes on methodology which have been approved by members in the CMA. The latest refers to the HS2017 changes (G/MA/366). Notes on methodology were also prepared for the HS2012 changes (G/MA/330 and Add.1), the HS2007 changes (G/MA/283) and the HS2002 changes (JOB(05)/124, Add.1 and Add.2).

CTS - Contents 

The CTS database contains all WTO members' concessions on goods in a standardized format. The database has been established as a working tool only, without implications as to the legal status of the information therein. All members' CTS files (approved and provisional) are available to members through the Tariff Analysis Online (TAO). For each WTO member there is a CTS file in MS-Access and text format containing its tariff commitments and, if applicable, its specific commitments in Agriculture.

The Concession table includes for each member the list of tariff commitments:

  • Bound MFN tariffs of the latest concession at the national customs tariff line level (HS-8 digits or more)

  • Base MFN tariffs used as the base rate in the most recent negotiations at the national customs tariff line level (HS-8 digits or more) for concessions not fully implemented

  • Other duties and charges (ODCs)

  • Special safeguards (SSG) applicable to the tariff line for agricultural products only

  • Present and First instruments referring to the WTO/GATT legal instrument in which the present/first concession(s) on the product were established

  • Initial Negotiating Rights (Present and Earlier INRs) referring to the WTO member(s) holding INRs on the present concession and/or earlier concession.

  • Implementation period providing the starting year and the end year of the period for the concession to be fully implemented

For concerned members, additional tables present information on specific commitments in agriculture:

  • Domestic support commitments (Total AMS) are recorded on a per member basis

  • Export subsidies with export subsidy bound outlay level and quantity recorded at the tariff line level and linked to relevant tariff concessions

  • Tariff quotas with in-quota bound duty and quota quantity recorded at the tariff line level and linked to the out of quota bound duty

CTS â Sources of information

The CTS contains concessions made during several Rounds of negotiations and/or at different periods in time. Members commitments are spread into many different legal instruments (Schedules, etc). These instruments are presented in many different formats (Excel, txt, Word, paper) and in different nomenclatures over time which makes them difficult to use.

The different sources of information cover:

  • Pre-Uruguay round concessions

  • Uruguay round concessions

  • Plurilateral agreements such as pharmaceuticals, trade in civil aircraft, distilled spirits, ITA and ITA expansion

  • HS transpositions (HS1996, HS2002, HS2007, HS2012)

  • Nairobi package to eliminate export subsidies

  • Accession of new members

  • Renegotiations through Articles XXIV and XXVIII

  • Rectifications and modifications of schedules

Jargon buster
  • ad valorem (AV): a tariff rate charged as percentage of the price, in contrast to non ad valorem duty which can be based on certain unit of measure (e.g., $5/kg).
  • applied rates: duties that are actually charged on imports. These can be below the bound rates.
  • bound rates (tariff binding): commitment not to increase a rate of duty beyond an agreed level. Once a rate of duty is bound, it may not be raised without compensating the affected parties
  • Harmonized System: World Customs Organizationâs international standard of coding used to identify products. Moreâ¦
  • MFN (most-favoured-nation) tariff: normal non-discriminatory tariff charged on imports (excludes preferential tariffs under free trade agreements and other schemes or tariffs charged inside quotas) from other WTO members
  • schedules: (for goods) list of bound tariff rates
  • tariff line (TL): the code assigned to each product based on the HS. It should have at least six digits which is the most detailed standard HS code. Additional digits can be added by a trading economy to further classify its own products. National tariff lines usually have 8-digits.
